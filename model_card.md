# 🎧 Model Card: Music Recommender Simulation

## 1. Model Name  

**Master of Genre**

---

## 2. Intended Use  

This model was created to explore the use of Generative AI and creating a music recommender based on a limited data set.

---

## 3. How the Model Works  

The recommender computes a score 40% based on genre, 25% based on mood match, 20% based on energy proximity, and 15% on acousticness preference match.

## 6. Limitations and Bias 

Where the system struggles or behaves unfairly. 

**Discovered Weakness — Genre Weight Creates a Filter Bubble:**
The genre signal carries 40% of the total score, which is large enough to override a user's other preferences entirely.
During testing with a user profile that combined `genre: ambient` with `mood: intense` and `energy: 0.9`, the system surfaced *Spacewalk Thoughts* — an ambient track with energy of 0.28 — at rank #3, purely because the genre label matched.
This means a user who wants high-energy music can still receive a slow, atmospheric song just because it shares a genre tag, which is the definition of a filter bubble: the system locks onto one signal and ignores contradicting evidence.
The problem is amplified by catalog depth — 13 of the 20 songs represent unique genres, so users whose preferred genre appears only once in the dataset receive one genre-bonus result and then fall back on weaker continuous signals for the remaining recommendations.
A more balanced approach would lower the genre weight or apply a minimum energy threshold so that genre matches that sharply contradict the user's energy or mood preference are penalized rather than rewarded.

## 7. Evaluation  

How you checked whether the recommender behaved as expected. 

### Profiles Tested

Six user profiles were run against the full 20-song catalog: High-Energy Pop, Chill Lofi, Deep Intense Rock, Genre Not in Catalog (Country), Conflicting Preferences (Ambient + Intense + High Energy), and All Midrange.
For each profile the top 5 results were reviewed to check whether the recommendations felt sensible and whether the scores reflected the strength of each match.

### What the Results Showed

The standard profiles behaved as expected — Chill Lofi produced a near-perfect top result (Library Rain at score 98), and Deep Intense Rock surfaced Storm Runner cleanly at the top.
The most surprising result came from the High-Energy Pop profile: *Gym Hero* ranked second with a score of 74 even though its mood is "intense," not "happy."
This happens because the genre bonus (40%) is powerful enough that any pop song — regardless of mood — starts with a large head start over songs from other genres that might actually feel happier.
In plain terms, imagine a friend who knows you like pop music, so they keep recommending pop songs even when you specifically said you are in a happy mood, not an intense one — that is what the system is doing.
The Conflicting Preferences profile revealed the filter bubble described in Section 6: a slow ambient track surfaced at rank 3 for a user who wanted high-energy intense music, simply because the genre tag matched.
The All Midrange profile produced the most compressed results — scores ranged from 30 to 59 — showing that when no strong categorical signal fires, the system struggles to separate good matches from mediocre ones.
